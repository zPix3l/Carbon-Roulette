# Method-Specific Carbon Credit Data for Game Design (2023–2025)

## Overview

This report compiles method-level data for major voluntary carbon credit project types, focusing on registry-approved methodologies, transaction price benchmarks, indicative sequestration/avoidance yields where robust data exist, and documented integrity risks in the voluntary carbon market (VCM).[^1][^2]
Only directly sourced numerical values are included; where no robust, method-specific number could be found, values are marked **UNVERIFIED — exclude from production use** as per the user requirement.[^1]

## Table 4 – Documented red flags and fraud / quality patterns in the VCM

The following entries summarize empirically documented issues in VCM project types, along with how they can often be detected or at least suspected from project documentation alone.

| Red‑flag pattern | Example report / case (name, year, author) | How it can appear in project documentation (detectable inconsistencies) |
|---|---|---|
| **Systematic over‑crediting in project‑level REDD+ (inflated baselines, weak counterfactuals)** | *Overstated carbon emission reductions from voluntary REDD+ projects in the Brazilian Amazon* (PNAS, 2020, West et al.) finds that 12 voluntary REDD+ projects overstated avoided deforestation relative to synthetic controls; credited reductions greatly exceeded empirically observed differences in deforestation.[^20][^21] The Integrity Council (ICVCM) and Ecosystem Marketplace also summarize 2023 debates around REDD+ baselines.[^1][^13] | Warning signs in a PDD: projected deforestation rates far above recent historical trends for the reference region; vague or weak justification for drastic future increases in deforestation pressure; small or no discussion of overlapping protected‑area policies; very high claimed tCO₂/ha/yr savings with little site‑specific evidence.[^20][^98] Cross‑check: compare project baseline deforestation to national or regional deforestation statistics and independent land‑use models. |
| **Non‑additional renewable‑energy projects (would have been built anyway)** | World Bank and EM analyses of renewable‑energy offsets show that many CDM/VCM RE projects were economically viable without carbon revenue, especially after cost declines in wind/solar; several policy analyses argue that such projects are non‑additional.[^81][^99] | PDD indicates large, grid‑connected wind or solar in middle‑ or high‑income markets where the technology is already mainstream, but financial additionality section shows high IRR even without carbon revenue; supportive policies (feed‑in tariffs, auctions) are ignored or down‑played.[^81] Also, baseline scenario assumes continued use of highly carbon‑intensive grid electricity with no policy‑driven decarbonization. |
| **Leakage and permanence risks in REDD+ (deforestation shifts elsewhere; fires or political change reverse gains)** | Multiple investigations, including *Overstated carbon emission reductions…* (West et al., 2020), the 2023 *Guardian/Die Zeit/SourceMaterial* investigation into Verra REDD+ projects, and Carbon Market Watch’s 2024 report *False friends – Why UN‑REDD and Article 6 carbon markets are incompatible*, highlight leakage and permanence issues.[^20][^21][^100][^16] | PDD shows limited or no quantitative leakage assessment (e.g., no modelling of agricultural expansion moving outside the project boundary); buffer pool size seems small relative to fire/storm risks; forest‑fire history and political drivers of deforestation are not discussed.[^16][^101] Long crediting periods with minimal interim baseline updates are also a warning sign. |
| **Over‑optimistic soil‑carbon baselines and permanence assumptions** | Comparative reviews of soil‑carbon codes (e.g., global analysis of farmland soil‑carbon codes, 2022) and OECD’s 2022 paper on soil‑carbon sequestration warn that some schemes assume high, sustained SOC gains (e.g., 4 per 1000) without accounting for saturation and reversibility.[^67][^64][^102][^103] | PDDs or programme documents assume constant annual SOC increases over decades at the high end of literature ranges (e.g., ≥3–5 tC/ha/yr) without tapering; limited measurement frequency; weak or absent provisions for monitoring reversals (tillage, drought, land‑use change) or for discounting temporary storage.[^66][^104] |
| **Household device projects (cookstoves) overstating usage and fuel savings** | Empirical work on cookstove adoption (e.g., experimental evidence on improved stove promotion in India and Kenya) shows that real‑world usage often falls short of lab expectations, and that many households continue to use traditional stoves (“stove stacking”).[^31][^41] Quality‑assurance bodies like Gold Standard have progressively tightened cookstove methodologies in response.[^34][^40] | PDD uses high default values for baseline fuel consumption and stove efficiency improvements, with minimal or outdated field survey data; monitoring plans rely on infrequent or very small user surveys; no accounting for stove stacking; unrealistic assumptions of 100% adoption and daily usage.[^34][^37] |
| **Hidden double counting or double claiming of emission reductions** | ICVCM’s Core Carbon Principles and accompanying Assessment Framework highlight risks of double claiming where host‑country NDC accounting and voluntary credits are not aligned, especially under Article 6.[^13][^1] | Project documents or registry entries lack clarity on whether host‑country corresponding adjustments have been (or will be) applied for credits used toward international targets; national inventory or Article 6 registry entries show the same reductions counted toward NDCs while credits are marketed as “offsets” for foreign buyers.[^13] |
| **Lack of transparency in project boundaries and activities (hard to verify what is actually happening)** | Nature‑based offsets data initiatives (e.g., Forest Carbon Project Boundaries / OffsetsDB) were created precisely because many registry projects lacked clear, machine‑readable spatial boundaries, making independent verification difficult.[^87][^88] | PDDs and registry entries provide vague maps or only textual descriptions of project areas; coordinates are coarse or missing; land‑tenure and rights of Indigenous or local communities are poorly documented.[^87][^105] This makes it difficult to cross‑check deforestation or land‑use change with satellite data. |
| **Social and human‑rights harms (e.g., REDD+ projects restricting customary land use)** | Case studies such as *“Saving the forest” with a REDD+ project: socio‑ecological repercussions on Indigenous people in Cambodia* (2024) document REDD+ projects that restrict access to land and resources for Indigenous communities without adequate participation or benefit sharing.[^106][^107] | PDDs describe restrictions on hunting, fuelwood collection, or shifting cultivation but provide little detail on consultation, compensation, or benefit sharing; free, prior and informed consent (FPIC) is asserted but not documented; grievance mechanisms are generic or absent.[^106][^108] |
| **Conflicts between UN‑REDD frameworks and carbon‑market additionality / crediting rules** | Carbon Market Watch’s *False friends – Why UN‑REDD and Article 6 carbon markets are incompatible* (2024) argues that mixing UN‑REDD finance with market crediting can lead to double counting and misaligned incentives.[^16] | Project descriptions rely heavily on UN‑REDD or other public finance yet still claim large volumes of marketable emission reductions without clear separation of what is paid via results‑based finance versus voluntary buyers.[^16] |

These patterns can be used to design in‑game “due diligence” mechanics where players must spot inconsistencies or missing information in project descriptions.
