# Forest-Based Carbon Sequestration, Water Co-Benefits, Monitoring Methods, and Known Integrity Issues

## Overview

This report compiles indicative quantitative ranges and qualitative evidence on carbon sequestration, hydrological and ecosystem-service co-benefits, monitoring and verification methods, and documented integrity issues for forest-based carbon projects with dual climate–water objectives. Values are necessarily generalized and vary substantially by site, stand age, management, and methodology; all numbers should be treated as order-of-magnitude indicators rather than project-ready parameters.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5]

**Important caveats.** Carbon sequestration rates here are net ecosystem production (NEP) or above‑ground biomass increments converted to approximate tonnes of carbon dioxide per hectare per year (tCO₂/ha/year), using a factor of 3.67 from tonnes of carbon. Hydrological metrics are highly context‑specific; only well‑supported generalizations and ranges are included.[^4][^5]

| **1. Tropical rainforest** | Intact mature tropical forests often show NEP around 1–6 t C/ha/year, equivalent to roughly 3.7–22 tCO₂/ha/year [IPCC, 1999][^4][^1]. Secondary/regenerating tropical forests commonly accumulate 1.3–3.0 t C/ha/year (≈ 4.8–11 tCO₂/ha/year) in above‑ground biomass [Poorter et al., 2016][^2]. | Meta‑analysis of forest canopies globally finds median rainfall interception of around 20–30% of gross precipitation, with humid evergreen forests at the upper end of this range [Zhong et al., 2024][^6]. One study in intact Indonesian peat swamp reported a net carbon sink of about 1.6 t C/ha/year (≈ 5.9 tCO₂/ha/year) after accounting for CO₂, CH₄, and DOC fluxes [Melling et al., 2005][^16]. | Peatlands act as long‑term water stores, maintaining high water tables and buffering downstream flow regimes; drainage typically increases peak flows and reduces baseflows [Joosten & Clarke, 2002][^17]. Quantitative species‑per‑hectare benchmarks are site‑specific and not globally standardized (UNVERIFIED). | Coastal lowlands of Indonesia (Kalimantan, Sumatra, Papua), Malaysia (Sarawak, Sabah), Brunei, Papua New Guinea, Congo Basin peatlands (DRC, Republic of Congo), boreal peatlands in Canada, Russia, and Fennoscandia.[^17][^20] |
| **3. Mangrove forests** | Global syntheses indicate mangrove soil carbon sequestration rates around 1.3 t C/ha/year (≈ 4.8 tCO₂/ha/year) on average [Alongi, 2014][^5]. Stand‑level studies of young or restored mangroves report higher total ecosystem sequestration, with estimates of roughly 23.8–38.5 tCO₂/ha/year for planted mangrove belts in some tropical sites [Sitoe et al., 2014][^21]. | Mangroves attenuate waves and storm surges; a mangrove belt on the order of several hundred metres width can substantially reduce wave heights and storm energy, lowering coastal flooding and erosion risk [Spalding et al., 2014][^22]. Root systems trap sediments and improve near‑shore water quality by filtering nutrients and pollutants [Alongi, 2008][^23]. Mangroves also influence local hydrology by slowing tidal flows and providing nursery habitat for fish and invertebrates.[^23] | Global assessments list roughly 70 true mangrove species worldwide [Tomlinson, 2016][^23]. Site‑level inventories commonly record 10–24 mangrove tree species per landscape in Southeast Asia and the Coral Triangle [Agduma et al., 2023][^24]. Local stand densities can exceed 1,000 stems/ha for dominant species such as *Rhizophora mucronata* [Bitantos et al., 2017; Jumawan, 2023][^25][^26]. | Tropical and subtropical coasts, especially Southeast Asia (Indonesia, Philippines, Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam), South Asia (India, Bangladesh Sundarbans), East Africa (Kenya, Tanzania, Mozambique), West Africa, northern Australia, Caribbean and Central American coasts.[^23][^24] |
| **4. Temperate deciduous forest** | IPCC syntheses report temperate deciduous forests with NEP typically in the range of about 2.5–7 t C/ha/year, equal to roughly 9.2–25.7 tCO₂/ha/year, depending on age and management [IPCC, 1999][^4]. Long‑term eddy‑covariance studies in European broadleaf forests find similar magnitudes, with annual net uptake often around 3–6 t C/ha/year (≈ 11–22 tCO₂/ha/year) in productive stands [Luyssaert et al., 2007][^4]. | Temperate broadleaf forests intercept around 20–40% of annual rainfall through canopy processes, with some conifer‑broadleaf mixtures reporting interception fractions above 40–50% of gross rainfall [Zhong et al., 2024; Crockford & Richardson, 2000][^6][^27]. Forested catchments generally show reduced flood peaks, higher baseflows, and lower sediment yields than comparable agricultural catchments, especially where riparian buffers are intact [Andréassian, 2004][^28]. | Species richness per hectare is lower than in tropical forests but still substantial; temperate broadleaf plots often contain on the order of 10–30 native tree species/ha in diverse regions such as parts of eastern North America, Europe, and East Asia [Wells et al., 2013][^29]. Overall biodiversity (including understory plants, fungi, and fauna) is high, especially in old‑growth stands.[^20] | Eastern North America (USA, Canada), Western and Central Europe, Caucasus, parts of East Asia (China, Japan, Korea), Southern South America (Chile, Argentina) in suitable climatic zones.[^29][^20] |
| **5. Boreal forest** | A long‑term study of Canadian boreal forest landscapes reported cumulative net ecosystem production of roughly 173 t C/ha over 138 years, averaging about 1.25 t C/ha/year (≈ 4.6 tCO₂/ha/year) when fire and regrowth cycles are included [Kurz et al., 2008][^1]. Primary boreal forests typically store very large carbon stocks (vegetation plus soil and peat often exceeding 300–1,000 t C/ha in some systems), but annual net sequestration is modest and highly disturbance‑driven [Woodwell Climate, 2020][^20]. | Boreal forests and peatlands regulate freshwater storage and flow across northern landscapes, with large soil and peat water reserves moderating runoff and supporting major river systems [Woodwell Climate, 2020][^20]. Snow interception by conifer canopies and delayed melt influence seasonal hydrographs, while permafrost thaw and wildfire are increasingly altering hydrology and water quality (e.g., dissolved organic carbon pulses) under climate change [Anderegg et al., 2025][^30]. | Boreal forests have relatively low tree species richness—often fewer than 10 dominant tree species at landscape scale—but support many specialized understory plants, lichens, and fauna, with high endemism in some taxa [Wells et al., 2013][^29]. Quantitative tree‑species‑per‑hectare benchmarks are typically in the single‑ to low‑double‑digit range (UNVERIFIED). | Canada and Alaska, Fennoscandia (Norway, Sweden, Finland), Russia’s boreal belt, parts of northern Mongolia and northern Japan.[^20][^15] |
| **6. Agroforestry systems** | Meta‑analyses compiled by the IPCC and others indicate that agroforestry systems in temperate and tropical regions often sequester on the order of 1.5–7.0 tCO₂/ha/year more than comparable treeless agriculture, depending on system design and baseline [Nair et al., 2010; IPCC, 2019][^31]. Case studies report annual sequestration of roughly 2.4 tCO₂/ha/year in European alley‑cropping systems and 0.5–3.5 tCO₂/ha/year in smallholder agroforestry in West Africa [Kay et al., 2019][^32][^33]. | Agroforestry can improve infiltration and reduce runoff compared to conventional monocultures, lowering erosion and enhancing soil moisture storage; tree rows or shade trees also reduce evaporative demand at the soil surface and can improve dry‑season water availability for crops in some contexts [Nair et al., 2010][^31]. Hydrological impacts are highly system‑specific and can be negative for downstream water yield where tree densities are high and water is limiting [Ellison et al., 2017][^9]. | Species richness varies widely by design, from simple windbreak systems with one or two tree species to highly diverse multistrata systems that approach forest‑like tree and crop diversity (dozens of species per farm) [Nair et al., 2010][^31]. No single standardized tree‑species‑per‑hectare benchmark exists across agroforestry types (UNVERIFIED). | Widespread: silvopastoral and alley‑cropping systems in Europe and North America; homegardens and shade coffee/cocoa in Latin America, Africa, and Asia; parkland agroforestry in the Sahel; multistrata systems in South and Southeast Asia.[^31] |
| **7. Bamboo plantations / bamboo agroforestry** | Fast‑growing bamboo stands (e.g., *Phyllostachys edulis*) in subtropical China have been reported to sequester roughly 8–12 t C/ha/year in above‑ground biomass during rapid growth, equivalent to about 29–44 tCO₂/ha/year, though rates decline as stands mature [Yue et al., 2010][^34]. Other studies report total ecosystem sequestration on the order of 10–30 tCO₂/ha/year for intensively managed bamboo plantations [Yiping et al., 2010][^35]. | Bamboo stands improve infiltration and reduce surface erosion on sloping land, stabilizing soils via dense rhizome and root systems; they can increase soil organic matter and water‑holding capacity relative to degraded cropland [Yiping et al., 2010][^35]. Hydrological impacts on catchment‑scale water yield are less well quantified and can be context‑dependent (UNVERIFIED). | Species richness in monoculture bamboo plantations is low (typically one planted bamboo species plus understory), but bamboo agroforestry systems that integrate trees and crops can host dozens of associated plant species per hectare and support substantial bird and invertebrate diversity [Yiping et al., 2010][^35]. Standardized biodiversity metrics for bamboo plantations are sparse (UNVERIFIED). | Subtropical and tropical Asia (China, India, Vietnam, Thailand), parts of East Africa and Latin America where bamboo has been introduced or occurs naturally.[^35][^34] |

## Table 2 – Monitoring and Verification Methods for Forest Carbon and Water/Ecosystem Services

Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) of forest carbon projects typically combines several methods, with trade‑offs among accuracy, cost, and scalability. Major standards such as Verra’s Verified Carbon Standard (VCS), Gold Standard, ART TREES, and UNFCCC mechanisms rely on IPCC Good Practice Guidance, which emphasizes ground plots, allometric equations, and increasingly remote sensing (optical, radar, LiDAR) to estimate activity data and emission factors.[^36][^37][^38][^39]

### Overview of tools and methods

| Method | What it measures / typical use | Accuracy and limitations | Cost range (very approximate) | Use in standards / programs |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Satellite imagery (e.g., Landsat, Sentinel, Planet)** | Optical and radar satellite data are used to map forest cover, deforestation and degradation, land‑use change, canopy greenness (NDVI and related indices), and, when calibrated with field or LiDAR data, above‑ground biomass and carbon stocks.[^40][^41] Landsat (30 m) and Sentinel‑2 (10 m) time series underpin most global and national forest monitoring systems, while higher‑resolution constellations (e.g., Planet) can resolve fine‑scale canopy disturbance.[^42][^38] | For forest‑non‑forest change, medium‑resolution imagery can achieve systematic errors on the order of 5–20% when using appropriate classification methods and ground data.[^36] Biomass estimation using optical plus radar data commonly attains coefficients of determination (R²) around 0.5–0.8 with RMSE of ≈10–30 Mg/ha in many case studies, but saturates at high biomass and is sensitive to cloud cover, sensor differences, and algorithm choice.[^40][^43][^44] Attribution of change (e.g., selective logging versus fire or storm) and detection of subtle degradation remain challenging.[^45][^38] | Open‑access imagery from Landsat and Sentinel is free; monitoring costs arise from processing, ground calibration, and capacity building. REDD+ monitoring cost assessments report total monitoring expenses ranging roughly from 0.5 to 550 USD/km² depending on precision requirements, combining satellite and ground inventory costs [Herold & Skutsch, 2009][^36]. Commercial high‑resolution data (e.g., Planet, Maxar) and analytics platforms add additional licensing and service costs that vary widely by area and product (UNVERIFIED). | Accepted across major standards: UNFCCC national REDD+ MRV, IPCC‑aligned national inventories, Verra REDD+ and ARR methodologies (e.g., VM0007, VM0047) for baseline setting and monitoring, ART TREES for jurisdictional baselines, Gold Standard Land Use & Forests (LUF) activity requirements, and many compliance and voluntary schemes.[^39][^46][^47][^48] Remote sensing is increasingly embedded in dynamic performance benchmarks and third‑party ratings of project performance.[^46][^49] |
| **2. Airborne / spaceborne LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)** | Airborne laser scanning (ALS) and spaceborne LiDAR missions such as NASA’s GEDI measure three‑dimensional forest structure (canopy height, vertical profile), which can be calibrated to plot biomass and carbon using allometric equations.[^50][^51] ALS provides high‑resolution wall‑to‑wall top‑of‑canopy height and structural metrics over project‑scale areas; GEDI provides sampled footprints that can be fused with optical/radar data for larger‑scale mapping.[^52][^50] | Airborne LiDAR calibrated with field plots can estimate stand‑level above‑ground biomass with R² often in the 0.6–0.8 range and relative RMSE around 15–35% for different biomass components in conifer forests.[^53][^54] Accuracy declines at very high biomass, for very heterogeneous canopies, and where ground plots are sparse or unrepresentative.[^53][^38] Airborne campaigns are sensitive to weather and require specialist processing; GEDI coverage is sampled rather than wall‑to‑wall and is being phased out as the mission ends.[^52][^50] | A forestry industry study in Australia estimated that updating forest inventories with traditional ground methods cost about 25–40 USD/ha, whereas LiDAR plus digital imagery could deliver comparable or better information for roughly 2–4 USD/ha at scale [FWPA, 2024][^55]. At project scale, commissioning bespoke ALS surveys typically implies total costs in the tens to hundreds of thousands of USD depending on area and terrain (UNVERIFIED). | Verra VCS methodologies allow LiDAR data to support biomass estimates, stratification, and baseline setting where quality criteria are met (e.g., VM0007 REDD+ modules and VM0047 for ARR).[^39][^46] LiDAR is also increasingly used by private rating agencies and initiatives (e.g., Sylvera’s "Biomass Atlas") to independently assess project biomass with reported errors below 10% at project scale.[^56] National forest inventories and research programs in Europe and North America also use ALS for management‑level inventories and calibration of satellite products.[^38] |
| **3. Drone (UAV) imagery + AI classification** | Multirotor or fixed‑wing drones equipped with RGB and sometimes multispectral or LiDAR sensors collect very high‑resolution imagery (centimetre‑scale) over sample blocks or small projects. Structure‑from‑motion photogrammetry or LiDAR point clouds can be used to derive tree heights, canopy cover, and, with allometric models, biomass. Deep learning models can classify tree crowns, detect gaps, and estimate stand parameters for carbon accounting and biodiversity monitoring.[^57][^58] | Drone‑based inventories can provide highly detailed structural data and crown‑level classification but are limited in spatial extent and require line‑of‑sight flight permissions and local operators.[^57] Accuracy depends on ground control points, sensor calibration, and the quality of AI models; strong performance has been demonstrated in research settings, but operational scaling and standardization are still emerging.[^57][^58] Cloud cover is less of an issue than for satellites, but flights can be hampered by rain, wind, and canopy closure. | One proposed deep‑learning-based forest inventory workflow reported traditional tropical ground inventories costing on the order of 300 USD/ha versus approximately 10 USD/ha for drone‑based mapping of 100 ha with a low‑cost quadrotor and local operator time [Lütjens et al., 2019][^57]. Hardware costs (drones, sensors) and data processing services add to this and vary widely by project size and region (UNVERIFIED). | Drones are widely used for project‑level monitoring (e.g., boundary checks, encroachment, canopy gaps) and as an auxiliary data source in many Verra and Gold Standard projects, but they are generally treated as supporting evidence within MRV rather than a standalone method. Guidance documents and MRV frameworks emphasise ensuring georeferencing quality and traceability when integrating UAV data.[^36][^38] |
| **4. Ground-based plot sampling (forest inventory plots)** | Permanent or temporary field plots are established to measure tree diameter at breast height (DBH), height, species, and sometimes wood density and health status.[^37][^59] These observations, combined with allometric equations, yield estimates of above‑ and below‑ground biomass and carbon per hectare. Plots also underpin estimates of dead wood, litter, soil carbon (with additional sampling), and biodiversity indicators. | Properly designed inventories are considered the most direct and accurate way to estimate stand biomass and carbon, particularly at small scales.[^37][^43] However, they are labour‑intensive, expensive, and often limited in spatial coverage. REDD+ monitoring cost studies report inventory‑related monitoring costs ranging from less than 10 USD/km² for large national systems up to several hundred USD/km² for intensive project‑level inventories, depending on access and precision requirements [Herold & Skutsch, 2009][^36]. Plot networks can be biased towards accessible areas, and measurement errors or inconsistent protocols can introduce systematic uncertainty.[^38][^60] | As above, national examples compiled for REDD+ monitoring show total monitoring costs (remote sensing plus inventories) between roughly 1.2 and 8.2 USD/km² for national forest monitoring assessments and up to about 400–550 USD/km² for intensive project‑level inventories in some tropical countries [Herold & Skutsch, 2009][^36]. Per‑hectare costs in rugged tropical forests can reach hundreds of USD when factoring transport and labour [Lütjens et al., 2019][^57]. | Ground plots are mandatory or strongly recommended across all major standards and IPCC guidance, forming the reference data for emission factors and calibration of remote sensing.[^37][^61] Verra, Gold Standard, ART TREES, and UNFCCC CDM/REDD+ frameworks all rely on statistically robust plot designs and remeasurement protocols for verification.[^39][^62][^47] |
| **5. IoT sensors (soil moisture, microclimate, fluxes, canopy conditions)** | Internet‑of‑Things (IoT) sensor networks in forests and peatlands measure variables such as soil moisture, temperature, humidity, soil CO₂ concentration, water table depth, and sometimes methane fluxes.[^63][^64] These data provide high‑frequency insights into ecosystem function, drought stress, fire risk, and greenhouse‑gas dynamics, complementing periodic plot measurements and remote sensing. Examples include low‑cost sensor networks in UK peatlands measuring temperature, humidity, soil moisture, CO₂ and CH₄ over thousands of records, and broader forest microclimate networks tracking responses to heat and drought.[^65][^66][^67] | IoT networks can achieve high temporal resolution and capture micro‑scale variability but are constrained by power supply, connectivity, sensor drift, and vandalism or wildlife damage.[^68][^67] Calibration against standard instruments is required, and coverage is typically limited to small footprints. These systems are excellent for research and early‑warning (e.g., fire risk) but are not yet widely standardized in carbon accounting methodologies, so their direct contribution to crediting tends to be indirect.[^68][^67] | Published examples describe “low‑cost” sensor nodes and networks but rarely provide comparable per‑hectare cost figures; costs depend on sensor types, communications (e.g., LoRaWAN, cellular, satellite), and deployment density.[^63][^69] As such, only qualitative statements (e.g., lower cost per observation than repeated manual visits once deployed) can be made at present (UNVERIFIED). | IoT sensors are not yet core to any major carbon standard’s default MRV protocols but are increasingly used in research projects, climate‑smart forestry pilots, and bespoke monitoring frameworks to evidence risk management (e.g., fire prevention, peat rewetting effectiveness).[^68][^67] Some standards (e.g., Gold Standard’s LUF requirements) allow innovative monitoring tools where properly justified and quality‑assured, which could include IoT networks.[^48] |
| **6. Allometric equations (e.g., FCOP-41 and related models)** | Allometric equations are mathematical models that estimate tree or stand biomass from easily measured dendrometric variables such as DBH, height, and wood density.[^37][^60] Pan‑tropical and regional equations (e.g., Chave et al. 2014 models) as well as species‑ or site‑specific equations (e.g., for African moist forests or Southeast Asian secondary forests) relate log‑transformed DBH, height, and wood density to above‑ground biomass with empirically fitted coefficients.[^70][^59][^71] Methodologies such as those used in Verra and other standards require projects to select, justify, and sometimes locally validate allometric models. | When well‑matched to the target forest type and diameter range, allometric equations can estimate individual‑tree biomass with typical uncertainties on the order of 10–30% at tree level.[^37][^60] Plot‑level uncertainties are often lower due to error cancellation across many trees.[^60] Mis‑specification of equations (e.g., using moist‑forest models in dry forests, or extrapolating beyond calibration DBH ranges) can introduce substantial bias.[^60][^61] Recent work emphasises selecting equations based on plot‑level performance rather than tree‑level fit to reduce uncertainty in total biomass estimates.[^60] | Equation development requires destructive sampling and is expensive; FAO guidance documents stress that once robust equations exist, they can be reused across many projects, making ongoing costs primarily those of field measurement rather than model building [FAO, 2012][^37]. No generic per‑hectare cost applies; costs are embedded in forest inventories and research campaigns (UNVERIFIED). | Allometric equations are the backbone of biomass and carbon estimation in IPCC guidance and all major forest carbon standards.[^37][^61] Verra, Gold Standard, ART TREES, and compliance schemes require documented, peer‑reviewed, or jurisdictionally approved allometric models and often provide hierarchy rules (use local species‑specific > regional > pan‑tropical default where available).[^61][^39][^62] FCOP‑type protocols and guidance documents (e.g., Winrock’s Allometric Equation Evaluation Guidance) provide standardized procedures for selecting and testing equations.[^61] |

## Table 3 – Documented Integrity Issues in Forest-Based Carbon Projects

Forest‑based carbon projects face recurring concerns about additionality, leakage, permanence, and baseline inflation. Recent investigative journalism, NGO reports, academic studies, and registry reviews document systemic problems and specific high‑profile cases.[^72][^73][^74]

### Patterns and cases of integrity risks

| Issue type | Pattern / description | Example cases and sources |
|---|---|---|
| **Additionality failures (forest not truly threatened or benefits overstated)** | Many avoided‑deforestation (REDD+) projects construct ex‑ante deforestation baselines that substantially exceed observed deforestation in appropriate counterfactual areas, leading to credits without real additional emission reductions.[^72] Statistical evaluations comparing enrolled project areas to matched control areas often find little or no difference in deforestation, implying that much of the credited “protection” would have occurred without the project.[^72] | A global analysis of tropical forest conservation offsets found that, across 26 REDD+ projects certified under major standards, only a minority showed significant reductions in deforestation relative to controls; where reductions existed, they were substantially smaller than credited, implying widespread over‑crediting and weak additionality [West et al., 2023][^72]. A nine‑month investigation by *The Guardian*, *Die Zeit*, and SourceMaterial concluded that more than 90% of rainforest credits from Verra‑certified REDD+ projects were likely “phantom credits” that did not represent real additional emission reductions, based on comparison of Verra baselines to independent scientific studies [Harvey et al., 2023][^73][^75]. A 2024 analysis of voluntary carbon market purchases by 20 large companies found that about 87% of purchased offsets carried high risk of not providing real and additional reductions, with forest projects being a major component [Trencher et al., 2024][^76]. Australian “human‑induced regeneration” (HIR) projects in rangelands have been found to show limited evidence of additional tree cover beyond what would be expected from rainfall variability and natural dynamics, raising additionality concerns under that scheme [Evans et al., 2024][^77]. |
| **Leakage (deforestation displaced elsewhere)** | Leakage occurs when avoided deforestation or reduced harvesting in a project area shifts land‑use pressure to other areas, eroding net climate benefit.[^78] Methodological reviews find that many REDD+ projects apply very low leakage deductions compared to values suggested in the scientific literature, or rely on simplistic regional assumptions, potentially under‑accounting indirect emissions.[^79] Leakage can be market‑mediated (e.g., timber demand shifting harvest to other forests) or activity‑shifting (e.g., deforestation moving just outside project boundaries). | A 2023 Carbon Market Watch report on REDD+ projects documented that projects often applied leakage deduction rates well below the 10–70% range supported by empirical and modelling studies, resulting in only small downward adjustments to credited emission reductions despite substantial risk of deforestation displacement [Carbon Market Watch, 2023][^79]. Reviews of forest offset protocols in North America highlight that improved forest management (IFM) projects that reduce harvests on enrolled lands can trigger increased harvesting elsewhere if overall wood demand is unchanged, yet protocols often treat such market leakage simplistically or omit it [Mitchell et al., 2008; CATF, 2025][^78][^74]. A 2025 analysis by The Nature Conservancy and collaborators argued that leakage varies widely by project type, biome, and existing practice adoption, and that one‑size‑fits‑all assumptions in current protocols are often inappropriate, calling for more nuanced and data‑driven leakage accounting [Belair et al., 2025][^80]. |
| **Permanence risk and reversals (fire, disease, harvest, climate stress)** | Forest carbon is inherently vulnerable to reversal through wildfire, pests, disease, drought, and land‑use change, whereas fossil CO₂ emissions are effectively permanent on human timescales.[^81] Offset schemes attempt to manage this risk through buffer pools and non‑permanence provisions, but recent analyses suggest that contributions have been under‑capitalized relative to increasing disturbance risks, especially wildfire in western North America.[^82][^83] | Analysis of California’s forest offset buffer pool using historical disturbance data concluded that wildfire alone had already consumed at least 95% of the credits set aside for all fire risks over 100 years within the program’s first decade, implying that the pool was severely undercapitalized and could not guarantee 100‑year permanence as intended [Badgley et al., 2022][^81][^82]. Detailed project‑level investigations by CarbonPlan document multiple offset projects in the US West suffering large wildfire‑induced carbon losses, including the Klamath East (ACR273) project, where the 2021 Bootleg Fire caused losses exceeding 3 million tCO₂ and triggered automatic project termination and retirement of more than 1.1 million buffer credits [CarbonPlan, 2022; CarbonPlan, 2023][^84][^83]. Legal and journalistic reporting on the Lionshead Fire and other large wildfires shows that some projects have been effectively destroyed, with slow or opaque disclosure of reversals and buffer‑pool drawdowns, undermining confidence in permanence management.[^85][^86] A 2025 study of non‑permanence risk tools used by forest offset protocols (including Verra’s) found that current buffer contributions do not adequately reflect increasing climate risks and allow reductions of up to 75% in contributions based on prevention claims, potentially underestimating real reversal risk [Anderegg et al., 2025][^87][^30]. |
| **Baseline inflation and methodological weaknesses** | Baseline inflation refers to constructing counterfactual deforestation or degradation trajectories that are too high, so that credited emission reductions exceed true performance. Methodological flexibilities—such as choice of reference regions, time windows, and modelling assumptions—can systematically bias baselines upward when project developers and registries have incentives to maximize credits.[^72][^79] | The same scientific study underlying *The Guardian*’s 2023 investigation showed that for a set of Verra REDD+ projects, registry‑approved baselines predicted far higher deforestation than observed in matched control areas, leading to average over‑crediting on the order of several hundred percent in many projects [West et al., 2023][^72]. The *Guardian*/*Die Zeit*/SourceMaterial investigation concluded that around 94% of rainforest credits from analysed Verra projects were “worthless,” attributing this largely to overstated baselines rather than to monitoring errors [Harvey et al., 2023; Project Censored summary, 2023][^73][^88][^89]. Verra’s subsequent revisions to REDD+ frameworks (e.g., REDD+MF VM0007 v1.8 and the new ARR methodology VM0047) explicitly acknowledge concerns about baseline setting and move towards dynamic performance benchmarks derived from remote sensing to reduce baseline inflation.[^39][^46] Carbon Market Watch’s “Error log” report catalogues methodological weaknesses in REDD+ baselines, including selective choice of high‑deforestation reference areas near roads or coasts to justify high projected deforestation in more remote, less threatened project areas [Carbon Market Watch, 2023][^79][^90]. |
| **Low‑quality demand and governance gaps in voluntary markets** | Even as methodological flaws are documented, many large corporate buyers have continued to purchase credits with high risk of non‑additionality or over‑crediting, creating demand for low‑integrity offsets and slowing reforms.[^76] Governance of voluntary markets remains fragmented, with multiple registries, rating agencies, and emergent initiatives seeking to tighten quality criteria but uneven adoption to date.[^91] | A 2024 study of 20 major corporate offset buyers found that 87% of their purchased credits between 2020 and 2023 were assessed as high‑risk for failing to deliver real, additional emission reductions, with forest projects a large share of these volumes [Trencher et al., 2024][^76]. The Clean Air Task Force’s 2025 scorecard of 20 forest carbon protocols in North America concluded that most protocols are currently too weak to ensure high‑quality credits, highlighting recurring issues in additionality tests, leakage accounting, permanence provisions, and monitoring requirements [CATF, 2025][^74]. Analytical and advocacy reports argue for stronger regulatory oversight, standardized high‑quality criteria (e.g., via the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market), and better integration of biodiversity and resilience metrics into forest crediting to avoid perverse outcomes.[^91][^74] |

## Notes on Use for Project Design and Due Diligence

1. **Site-specific measurement is essential.** The biome‑level ranges in Table 1 are not sufficient for project design or crediting; site‑specific inventories, long‑term monitoring, and calibrated remote sensing are required to quantify carbon and water outcomes.[^37][^36]
2. **Hydrological outcomes can be non‑linear and scale‑dependent.** While forests generally improve water quality and regulate flows at catchment scale, tree planting or protection can reduce water yield in dry regions; rigorous hydrological assessment is needed where water scarcity is an issue.[^9][^28]
3. **Methodological choices drive credit volume.** As documented in Table 3, baseline construction, leakage assumptions, and permanence provisions often matter more for credited volumes than raw sequestration rates; due diligence should focus heavily on these aspects and on how monitoring methods from Table 2 are actually implemented.[^79][^74][^72]
4. **Co-benefits and risks beyond carbon.** Biodiversity and ecosystem‑service outcomes (e.g., for water, livelihoods, and resilience) are central to the integrity of “dual impact” projects; yet many current standards have limited or heterogeneous biodiversity metrics, making independent ecological assessment important alongside carbon MRV.[^92][^20]
