# Water Credit Markets, Project Metrics, and Scarcity Baselines

## Executive overview

Voluntary water credit markets are emerging but remain fragmented, small in scale, and far less standardized than carbon markets. Existing mechanisms such as Gold Standard’s Water Benefit Certificates, Bonneville Environmental Foundation’s Water Restoration Certificates, and various water stewardship certifications define units of volumetric benefit or stewardship performance, but they are not yet widely traded commodities. For educational purposes, it is therefore useful to distinguish between (a) tradable volumetric certificates, (b) site-level stewardship certifications, and (c) broader accounting frameworks like Volumetric Water Benefit Accounting.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5]


## Table 1 – Current state of water credit / water benefit certificate markets

### Existence of standardized water credits

| Question | Evidence-based answer |
|---------|------------------------|
| Do standardized "water credits" exist? | A few standardized volumetric instruments exist, but they are niche and not yet analogous to large-scale carbon markets.[^1][^2] Gold Standard’s Water Benefit Certificates (WBCs) and Bonneville Environmental Foundation’s Water Restoration Certificates (WRCs) are the main examples of credit-like units linked to specific volumes of water supplied, conserved or restored; Alliance for Water Stewardship (AWS) certification and similar standards certify stewardship performance at sites but do not themselves issue freely tradable "credits".[^3][^4][^5] |
| Main volumetric instruments | 1) Gold Standard Water Benefit Certificates (WBCs); 2) BEF Water Restoration Certificates® (WRCs); 3) emerging positive water credit schemes such as Act4Water’s CAP units and watershed certificates like miPáramo.[^3][^4][^6][^7] |
| Key stewardship / accounting frameworks often confused with credits | Alliance for Water Stewardship (AWS) Standard; corporate water stewardship and water footprint standards (e.g., ISO 14046); Volumetric Water Benefit Accounting (VWBA) from WRI and partners, which defines methods and indicators to quantify water benefits consistently for stewardship activities and can underpin crediting but is not itself a market standard.[^4][^5][^8] |

### Gold Standard Water Benefit Certificates (WBCs)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Program description | Gold Standard’s Water Benefit Standard (WBS), launched around 2014, was the first globally consistent standard to certify the positive socio‑economic impacts of water projects; the associated Water Benefit Certificates represent quantified water benefits that can be purchased by companies and investors.[^1][^3][^9] The principles and safeguards of the WBS have since been integrated into Gold Standard’s broader impact registry and methodologies.[^9] |
| Unit of impact | In the Water Benefit Standard Requirements (beta), a Verified Water Benefit Certificate assures that a specific volume of water has been sustainably supplied, purified and/or conserved; for most projects 1 WBC corresponds to 1 cubic metre per year of water benefit, with alternative scalings for very large projects (e.g., 1 WBC = 100 m³/year above 1.3 million m³/year).[^3] |
| Methodologies | Gold Standard has a dedicated methodology for accreditation of water access projects that quantifies volumes of drinking water protected from contamination and used for drinking, cooking and food cleaning.[^10] Safe water supply projects in Gold Standard’s carbon portfolio (e.g., borehole rehabilitation, household and community treatment technologies) are covered by a separate safe drinking water methodology that links access to safe water to avoided emissions from boiling water; updated annexes align parameters with WHO WASH guidance and specify water quality testing, usage rates, leakage assumptions and baseline conditions.[^11][^12] |
| Project pipeline / pilots | Public communications describe the Water Benefit Standard as being tested through pilot projects (for example in Uganda and Ghana) with an aspirational goal of 200 Water Benefit projects by 2020 benefiting over 3 million people, but there is limited evidence that this target was reached and the dedicated WBS registry now lists only a small number of projects with Water Benefit Certificate issuances.[^1][^2][^13] Gold Standard’s own Water Benefit Standard Registry page confirms that it tracks WBCs issued to projects under the legacy standard but does not publish an easily machine-readable project count.[^2] |
| Registry / framework | Gold Standard maintains a Water Benefit Standard Registry and is in the process of integrating WBC tracking into its unified Impact Registry; the registry records issuance and retirement of WBCs similarly to carbon credits but on a much smaller scale.[^2][^9] |

### Other water credit / water stewardship systems

| System | Type | How it works | Notes |
|--------|------|--------------|-------|
| BEF Water Restoration Certificates® (WRCs) | Volumetric certificates | Each BEF WRC represents 1,000 gallons of water restored to critically dewatered ecosystems; projects are verified by the National Fish and Wildlife Foundation, and WRCs are used by companies and building projects to "offset" their water use or earn LEED pilot credits.[^4][^14][^15] |
| BEF WRC pricing | Market indication | Current retail offerings show WRCs being sold at about 4 USD per 1,000 gallons (roughly 1.06 USD per m³); earlier coverage described them at around 1 USD per 1,000 gallons, suggesting price increases over time and variation by channel.[^4][^16][^17] |
| LEED Pilot Credit WEpc110 | Use of WRCs in building certification | Under USGBC’s LEED BD+C and ID+C pilot credit WEpc110, projects can earn an innovation point by purchasing WRCs from BEF to match 100–200% of their modeled annual water budget for a 5‑year contract, with different purchase levels depending on whether the WRCs originate inside or outside the project watershed.[^15] |
| Alliance for Water Stewardship (AWS) Standard | Site‑level certification | The AWS Standard is a globally applicable framework for major water users to understand their water use, assess shared catchment risks and implement a five‑step water stewardship plan; third‑party certification (Core, Gold, Platinum) confirms that a site meets performance criteria but does not itself generate tradable volumetric credits.[^4][^18][^19] |
| Volumetric Water Benefit Accounting (VWBA) | Accounting framework | VWBA, developed by WRI and partners, provides standardized indicators and calculation methods to quantify volumetric water benefits (e.g., m³ of water saved, replenished or pollution reduced) for stewardship activities; the framework is designed to support consistent tracking and comparison of corporate water stewardship investments and is explicitly positioned as an underpinning for site certifications and certificates like WBCs and WRCs.[^8][^20] |
| Positive Water Credits (Act4Water) | Emerging credit standard | The Act4Water standard defines Positive Water Credits (CAP) where 1 CAP equals 1,000 m³ of water footprint saved, based on either ISO water footprinting or similar methods; the scheme aims to aggregate actions that reduce water footprints and issue standardized credit units.[^7] |
| Watershed restoration certificates (e.g., miPáramo) | Watershed PES linked to certificates | Programs such as miPáramo in the Andes have been described as Water Benefit Certificate initiatives that channel performance‑based finance into conservation of high‑Andean wetlands, using the WBS concepts to quantify conserved or regulated water volumes.[^21] |

### Typical price range for water benefit certificates / water credits

| Instrument | Indicative price range | Evidence / caveats |
|-----------|------------------------|---------------------|
| BEF WRCs | Approximately 1–4 USD per 1,000 gallons of restored flow (about 0.26–1.06 USD per m³), depending on buyer, channel and year.[^4][^16][^17][^22] | A 2011 article noted that WRCs cost about 1 USD to restore 1,000 gallons of water; more recent retail offerings list a price of 4 USD per 1,000 gallons.[^16][^4] These are retail prices rather than exchange‑traded market prices. |
| Gold Standard WBCs | No robust public market price data found – transactions are typically bespoke corporate procurements bundled with broader sustainability partnerships. | Secondary literature on water‑related carbon and water certificates notes that WBCs have not generated scaled market demand beyond a limited set of corporate buyers, and prices are not widely disclosed.[^1][^2][^23] |
| Other water credits (positive water credits, watershed certificates) | Prices are generally not disclosed publicly; most pilots operate via negotiated contracts or as internal accounting rather than open markets. | Emerging schemes such as Positive Water Credits define credit units (e.g., per 1,000 m³) but are still at early stages of adoption with limited transparent pricing.[^7][^21] |

### Organizations actively developing water credit standards and accounting

| Organization | Role in water credits / accounting |
|--------------|-----------------------------------|
| Gold Standard Foundation | Developed the Water Benefit Standard and Water Benefit Certificates, maintains the WBC registry, and continues to develop water‑related methodologies for safe water access and other interventions within its impact certification framework.[^3][^10][^11][^12] |
| Bonneville Environmental Foundation (BEF) | Originator and operator of Water Restoration Certificates® used to fund flow restoration projects in US watersheds; WRCs are referenced in LEED and in WRI’s VWBA as a key example of volumetric water benefit instruments.[^4][^14][^15][^20] |
| Alliance for Water Stewardship (AWS) | Maintains the AWS Standard and associated certification requirements (most recently updated certification requirements in 2024) that define globally recognized water stewardship performance at site level.[^4][^18][^19] |
| World Resources Institute (WRI) and partners | Leads Aqueduct water risk analytics and co‑developed Volumetric Water Benefit Accounting, which provides a methodological backbone for quantifying water benefits and linking them to certificates and stewardship claims.[^8][^20][^24] |
| UN‑Water / UNESCO / UN World Water Assessment Programme | Through the World Water Development Reports and SDG monitoring, UN‑Water bodies shape the global framing of water scarcity, water risks and the need for better metrics, which in turn influence emerging water credit and stewardship frameworks.[^24][^25] |
| Regional / thematic initiatives | Examples include water credit proposals in India, which discuss project‑based water credit systems and highlight challenges such as data constraints and regulatory ambiguity, and watershed payment programs documented in watershed PES surveys.[^6][^22][^26] |


## Table 2 – Water project types and measurable metrics

### 1. Clean water access / water ATMs / kiosks for underserved populations

| Dimension | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Primary unit of impact | Litres of safe water delivered per person per day, or total m³/year of safe water provided to a defined beneficiary population.[^10][^27] |
| Realistic service levels (L/person/day) | Humanitarian guidance from WHO and partners indicates that 7.5 L/person/day is a bare survival minimum; 15 L/person/day is the minimum emergency target; and about 20 L/person/day is needed to cover basic drinking, cooking and hygiene needs.[^27][^28] Case studies of informal settlements and small towns show many households using 5–35 L/person/day from piped or kiosk systems, often below recommended levels; for example, in Ghana, rural piped systems provided 5–35 L/person/day while the national and JMP minimum standard for "basic access" was 20 L/person/day.[^29] In a Mumbai slum, 95% of residents used less than the WHO‑recommended minimum of 50 L/person/day.[^30] A GTZ water kiosk case study found that consumers in low‑income areas typically collected about 20 L/person/day from kiosks at regulated prices.[^31] |
| Typical TDS / turbidity targets for kiosk water | WHO drinking‑water guidelines emphasize microbiological safety and acceptability; TDS is managed mainly for taste, with palatability usually acceptable below about 1,000 mg/L, and turbidity should be below 5 NTU and preferably below 1 NTU to ensure effective disinfection.[^32][^33][^34] Water kiosk systems using filtration and UV disinfection are designed to meet WHO guideline values for microbial parameters and key chemicals.[^35] |
| Existing certification / verification frameworks | Gold Standard methodologies for water access and safe drinking water define monitoring of volumes delivered, water quality (meeting WHO or national standards), access distance, and usage patterns; these can be used to issue Water Benefit Certificates and/or carbon credits.[^10][^11][^12] National water quality regulations and surveillance programs apply in most countries, and household‑ and community‑level water treatment technologies may also be evaluated against WHO performance criteria.[^36] |
| Countries where projects are common | Community water kiosks, ATMs and small water enterprises are documented extensively in Ghana and other West African countries, India (urban and rural water ATMs), and Kenya (Nairobi slums), as well as in other parts of Sub‑Saharan Africa and South Asia.[^31][^35][^37][^38] |

### 2. Water treatment and purification (quality improvements)

| Dimension | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Primary units of impact | Reductions in turbidity (NTU), total dissolved solids (mg/L), and specific contaminants such as arsenic, fluoride, nitrate, iron, and microbiological indicators (E. coli / thermotolerant coliforms per 100 mL) relative to WHO guideline values.[^32][^33][^39] |
| Realistic ranges – turbidity | Studies of untreated or poorly treated groundwater and piped supplies in cities such as Yenagoa (Nigeria) and other locations report turbidity values ranging from about 20–40 NTU, well above the WHO 5 NTU aesthetic threshold.[^40] WHO guidance for safe drinking water recommends turbidity below 1 NTU before disinfection in higher‑resource systems, with a practical upper limit of 5 NTU in small or low‑resource supplies.[^33] Many treatment interventions therefore target reductions from tens of NTU to consistently below 1–5 NTU. |
| Realistic ranges – TDS | WHO does not set a health‑based guideline for TDS but notes that water with TDS below about 600 mg/L is generally considered good, and palatability decreases significantly above 1,000 mg/L.[^32][^34] Case studies of urban and rural drinking water often report TDS values between 50 and 500 mg/L, though salinity problems can push TDS much higher in some aquifers; a survey in Pakistan, for example, found TDS below 500 mg/L in most bottled waters and many municipal supplies but exceedances in some locations.[^41][^42] |
| Realistic ranges – microbial quality | WHO requires that E. coli or thermotolerant coliform bacteria be absent in any 100 mL of drinking water intended for human consumption.[^32][^36] Field studies of urban and rural supplies in Africa and Asia frequently find E. coli in a substantial fraction of samples, illustrating that many systems deliver water that meets quantity but not safety standards.[^36][^43] Treatment projects therefore often measure success as the proportion of samples meeting the 0/100 mL E. coli standard. |
| Certification / verification frameworks | WHO Guidelines for Drinking‑water Quality (4th edition with 1st and 2nd addenda) provide global guideline values; national standards (e.g., USEPA National Primary Drinking Water Regulations) specify enforceable limits for turbidity and contaminants; Gold Standard water and safe water methodologies require documented compliance with WHO or national standards at point of use.[^32][^33][^36][^11] |
| Countries where projects are common | Contaminant‑focused treatment (arsenic, fluoride, salinity) is common in South Asia and parts of Africa; turbidity and microbial control interventions occur worldwide but are particularly prevalent in low‑ and middle‑income countries with high groundwater or surface‑water contamination, such as Pakistan, Nigeria, Uganda and others.[^36][^39][^40][^43] |

### 3. Water distribution efficiency (leakage reduction, network optimization)

| Dimension | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Primary units of impact | Non‑revenue water (NRW) as a percentage of system input volume; physical losses (m³/year) avoided; improvements in continuity of supply and pressure.[^44][^45] |
| Realistic ranges – baseline and post‑project NRW | A World Bank review found that average NRW levels in developing‑country utilities are around 35%, with many systems significantly higher.[^44] Targeted interventions in Indonesian utilities reduced NRW from higher baselines down to about 15%, demonstrating that leakage reductions to the 15–25% range are achievable with sustained investment and performance‑based contracting.[^45] Global benchmarking exercises similarly document utilities operating anywhere from below 15% NRW to well over 40–50% in poorly maintained systems.[^44] |
| Certification / verification frameworks | There is no dedicated global "water efficiency credit" standard, but performance is commonly benchmarked using indicators such as NRW, Infrastructure Leakage Index and continuity of supply within utility benchmarking networks (e.g., IBNET); water efficiency and conservation are also embedded in green building rating systems and in AWS certification criteria for water use, loss reduction and catchment impact.[^4][^18][^46] |
| Countries where projects are common | NRW reduction and network optimization projects are widespread in rapidly urbanizing regions, including Southeast Asia (e.g., Indonesia), Latin America, South Asia and many African cities, where aging infrastructure and intermittent supply lead to high physical and commercial losses.[^44][^45] |

### 4. Water recycling and reuse (greywater, industrial reuse)

| Dimension | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Primary units of impact | Fraction of water demand met through recycled water (% reuse), or absolute volumes of wastewater reclaimed for beneficial use (m³/year); associated reductions in freshwater withdrawals.[^47][^48] |
| Realistic reuse levels | Industrial water reuse case studies and reviews indicate that advanced treatment technologies (e.g., membrane bioreactors, reverse osmosis) can enable reuse rates of up to 95% for specific industrial processes, dramatically reducing intake needs.[^47] Broader industrial circularity strategies that combine reduction, reuse and reclaim can deliver 75–90% water savings across sites.[^48] Municipal reuse programs in leading countries such as Singapore and Israel also demonstrate high municipal reuse fractions, but typical industrial and municipal projects in other regions may start with more modest targets of 20–50% reuse due to cost and infrastructure constraints.[^47][^48] |
| Certification / verification frameworks | Water reuse contributes to multiple credit frameworks rather than having a single dedicated credit standard: it is recognized in LEED water efficiency credits, in AWS site assessments (which look at consumptive use and discharges), and in local permitting and regulatory approvals.[^4][^18][^49] Some corporate water stewardship accounting frameworks (e.g., VWBA) treat reduced withdrawals through reuse as volumetric water benefits that can be quantified alongside other interventions.[^8] |
| Countries where projects are common | Industrial and municipal reuse is particularly advanced in water‑stressed, high‑income regions such as Israel, Singapore, parts of the United States (e.g., California, Arizona), Spain, and in industrial clusters worldwide; interest is growing rapidly in emerging economies as water scarcity and regulatory pressures increase.[^47][^48][^49] |

### 5. Watershed restoration / catchment protection

| Dimension | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Primary units of impact | Hectares of watershed restored or protected; additional groundwater recharge (m³/year); changes in groundwater levels (m/year); changes in baseflow or peak flow; sediment and nutrient load reductions.[^20][^50] |
| Realistic recharge / infiltration gains | Analyses of managed aquifer recharge (MAR) and nature‑based solutions report that well‑designed infiltration schemes can infiltrate more than 80% of inflow as groundwater recharge, and in some cases reverse long‑term groundwater declines of up to 2.5 m/year.[^50] Restoration of natural infiltration areas (e.g., infiltration basins, field infiltration, subsurface recharge wells) increases aquifer storage and can substantially reduce surface runoff.[^51] |
| Certification / verification frameworks | Watershed restoration projects are often funded via payments for ecosystem services (PES) and water funds, monitored through hydrological indicators and sometimes linked to volumetric accounting frameworks like VWBA or to Water Benefit Certificates; AWS certification also recognizes watershed‑scale collaboration and impact.[^8][^18][^21][^24] |
| Countries / regions where projects are common | Watershed restoration is prominent in Latin America (e.g., Andean páramo restoration), the western United States (instream flow restoration, groundwater recharge), and increasingly in parts of Africa and Asia where MAR and watershed rehabilitation are used to buffer climate variability.[^20][^21][^50][^51] |

### 6. Desalination projects

| Dimension | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| Primary units of impact | Volume of potable water produced (m³/day or m³/year); specific energy consumption (kWh/m³); share of local demand met by desalinated water.[^52] |
| Realistic energy consumption ranges | Thermodynamic analyses and reviews of seawater reverse osmosis (SWRO) show that the theoretical minimum energy for seawater (35,000 mg/L TDS, 50% recovery) is around 1.07 kWh/m³, but real full‑scale SWRO plants typically consume about 2.5–4.0 kWh/m³ for the RO process and around 3.5–4.5 kWh/m³ including pre‑ and post‑treatment.[^52][^53] |
| Certification / verification frameworks | Desalination plants are usually regulated by national drinking‑water standards for quality and by environmental permitting for brine discharge and energy use; they may participate in broader sustainability frameworks (e.g., environmental management certifications, AWS for site‑level stewardship) but do not typically generate dedicated "desalination credits".[^4][^18] |
| Countries where projects are most common | Large‑scale desalination is concentrated in the Middle East and North Africa (e.g., Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Israel), as well as in Spain, Australia and parts of the United States and other arid coastal regions.[^24][^52] |

### Economic cost of water scarcity

| Indicator | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| GDP impact by 2050 | The World Bank report "High and Dry: Climate Change, Water and the Economy" finds that in regions such as the Middle East, the Sahel in Africa, Central and South Asia, climate‑exacerbated water scarcity could reduce GDP growth by up to 6% by 2050 if no action is taken.[^66][^67] |
| Urban water availability | The same analysis warns that reduced freshwater availability and competing uses (energy, agriculture) could reduce water availability in some cities by as much as two‑thirds by 2050 compared to 2015 levels.[^66][^67] |

### Projected global water demand gap

| Projection | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| 2030 global water shortfall | United Nations World Water Development Reports and related UN communications warn that if current trends continue, the world could face a 40% shortfall in available water by 2030 – i.e., only about 60% of the water needed to meet projected demand will be available.[^68][^69][^70] |
| Drivers of the gap | The projected gap is driven by population growth (to around 9–10 billion by 2050), rising incomes, urbanization, and climate‑change‑induced variability, alongside persistent inefficiencies and pollution that reduce usable supplies.[^24][^68][^70] |


## Table 5 – Documented issues and fraud / integrity risks in water projects

### Overview

The nascent nature of water crediting and the complexity of water service delivery create distinct integrity risks compared with carbon markets. Documented issues span both project‑level failures and systemic challenges in measurement, monitoring and claims.

### Documented risk types and examples

| Risk / issue | Description and evidence |
|--------------|-------------------------|
| Overstating beneficiary counts and service levels | Evaluations of water technologies such as the PlayPump in southern Africa found that installed systems often failed to deliver the promised volumes and could not realistically meet the advertised number of beneficiaries, leading to under‑supply relative to recognized minimum water standards.[^71][^72] UNICEF reported cases where it took about 4 hours of continuous pumping to fill a 2,500‑L tank, corresponding to roughly 625 L/h versus the claimed 1,400 L/h; independent analyses concluded that it would be impossible for a single PlayPump to provide 25 L/day to the large populations sometimes claimed.[^71] Such cases illustrate how marketing claims can substantially overstate real service levels and beneficiary reach. |
| Water quality gaps – delivering water that is not safe | Multiple nationally representative or city‑level studies in Africa and Asia show that many "improved" or piped sources deliver water that fails to meet WHO microbial or chemical standards, even when infrastructure exists.[^36][^39][^40][^43] For example, surveys in urban Pakistan and elsewhere have found widespread exceedances of arsenic, fluoride or microbial indicators in drinking water, meaning that nominal access projects may not translate into safe water.[^36][^39] A global analysis of MDG water targets estimated that when microbial quality and sanitary risk are considered, about 1.8 billion people used unsafe water in 2010, far more than implied by infrastructure‑based "improved source" indicators.[^62] |
| Over‑crediting and parameter uncertainty in safe water carbon / water projects | Gold Standard’s internal quality assurance review of its Safe Water Supply (SWS) project portfolio identified a material risk of over‑estimation of emission reductions, leading to potential over‑issuance of credits in some projects, particularly borehole schemes.[^73] An independent investigation led by Berkeley Air Monitoring Group found that some monitored values (e.g., usage rates, boiling displacement, technology performance) were higher than expected based on academic literature and historic field research, even though developers had followed methodologies.[^73] This prompted Gold Standard to revise methodologies for safe water projects, tighten default values and monitoring requirements, and align more explicitly with WHO WASH guidance.[^11][^12] |
| Methodological concerns with "suppressed demand" approaches | Legal and human‑rights analyses of carbon‑financed water projects using "suppressed demand" (crediting avoided emissions from hypothetical future boiling) argue that such approaches may generate credits that are weakly linked to actual behavior and may not ensure that all dimensions of the human right to water (availability, accessibility, acceptability, safety, affordability) are met.[^74] These critiques highlight the risk that carbon or water credits are issued on the basis of optimistic assumptions rather than measured changes. |
| Data and monitoring constraints for water credits | Analyses of proposed water credit systems, for example in India, emphasize that limited data on water use, conservation measures, and project performance, along with monitoring challenges and regulatory ambiguity, make it difficult to design robust crediting schemes and can create room for over‑ or under‑estimation of benefits.[^6][^26] WRI’s VWBA framework similarly notes that without standardized methods and strong monitoring, volumetric benefit claims can be inconsistent and difficult to compare.[^8][^20] |
| Infrastructure fraud and non‑operational plants | Investigations by the European Anti‑Fraud Office (OLAF) into EU‑funded water and wastewater projects uncovered cases where water treatment plants were constructed but never actually operated, as well as submission of false documents and failures to meet eligibility criteria, resulting in recommendations to recover over 19.5 million EUR.[^75] Such cases show that even where capital investments are made, absence of operation and maintenance can render projects non‑functional while funds are still claimed as spent. |
| Procurement and financial manipulation in water projects | The Asian Development Bank has documented integrity risks and red flags in large water infrastructure procurements, including misrepresentation of bidders’ turnover and financial capacity, use of unaudited or incomplete financial statements, and inappropriate consideration of subcontractor contracts, all of which can lead to contracts being awarded to unqualified firms and raise the risk of project under‑performance.[^76] |
| "Water neutral" or "water positive" claims without robust impact measurement | Commentaries in business and sustainability literature argue that corporate claims of being "water neutral" or "water positive" are often misleading, since companies cannot fully eliminate their water footprints and offset projects may not be equivalent in location, timing or type of water impact.[^77] An investigative report into Coca‑Cola’s widely publicized claim to "replace every drop" of water it uses concluded that the company substantially overstated its water replenishment achievements by including projects whose benefits were hard to verify or not equivalent to production footprints.[^78] Such examples illustrate reputational risk and the need for rigorous, context‑specific accounting. |
| Double‑counting and overlapping service areas (conceptual risk) | Researchers working on global WASH monitoring note that traditional indicators based on infrastructure type can double‑count or mis‑attribute service levels when multiple providers or overlapping programs exist, and that multilevel modeling and improved survey methods are needed to better estimate true access.[^79][^80] While there are few publicly documented cases of deliberate double‑counting of beneficiaries in water credit schemes, the methodological literature highlights this as an important area of potential bias if not carefully controlled. |

### Implications for educational game design

For an educational game, these integrity issues can be translated into mechanics such as uncertain parameter ranges (usage rates, leakage, quality compliance), the need to invest in monitoring and verification, trade‑offs between capital expenditure and long‑term operation, and reputational risks from over‑stated claims. The cited cases provide real‑world narratives that can anchor scenarios about project success, failure and controversy in the water sector.[^71][^72][^73][^78]
